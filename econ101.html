Lecture 2 - 05/01/2018

Chapter 1 - Key Concepts in Economics

The Core Ideas Of Economics

All economic questions occur since we want more than we can get.
For example, we want more time than we can have.

Scarcity: our inability to satisfy all our wants

We must make choices since we face scarcity.
The choices we make depend on the incentives we face.

Incentive: a reward that encourages an action or a penalty that discourages an action


Standard Definition of Economics
  • Economics
    • the social science that studies the choices that individuals, businesses, governments, and entire societies make as they cope with scarcity and the incentives that influence and reconcile those choice
    • divides in to two main parts:
      • Microeconomics
      • Macroeconomics
  • Microeconomics
    • the study of choices that individuals and businesses make, the way those choices interact in markets, and the influence of governments
    • Example: Why are people buying more e-books and fewer hard copy books?
  • Macroeconomics
    • the study of the performance of the national and global economies
    • Example: What caused the recession of 2008?

Two Big Economic Questions
  1. How do choices end up determining what, how, and for whom goods and services get produced?
  2. When do choices made in the pursuit of self-interest also promote the social interest?

Goods: physical objects such as laptops and cellphones
Services: tasks performed for people such as internet cervice and cellphone service

What?
What we produce varies across countries and changes over time.

How?
How we produce is described by the technologies and resources that we use.
Goods and services are produced by using productive resources that economists call factors of production.

Factors of production are grouped into four categories:

  • Land
    • the "gifts of nature" that we use to produce goods and services
  • Labour
    • the work time and work effort that people devote to producing goods and services
  • Capital
    • the tools, instruments, machines, buildings, and other constructions that businesses use to produce goods and services
    • the knowledge and skill that people obtain from education, on-the-job training, and work experience are called human capital
    • money, stocks, and bonds are called financial cpaital
  • Entrepreneurship
    • the human resource that organizes land, labour and capital

Since capital includes most of the things, it may be easy to mess up with land.
For example, forest is a land but if we cut down the tree, the wood we get is a capital.
The oil beneath the Earth's surface is land, and if we recovered the oil, it is capital.

Example: Your neighbour has a bakery

  • Land: power, raw materials for making bread (flour, salt, water...)
  • Labour: the people who work in the bakery
  • Capital: the bakery, bread maker
  • Entrepreneurship: Your neighbour's skills at leading the bakery

For Whom?
Who gets the goods and services depends on the incomes that people earn.

  • Land earns rent.
  • Labour earns wages.
  • Capital earns interest.
  • Entrepreneurship earns profit.

Notice that interest is always with percentage, but rent, wages and profit are with money.

Lecture 3 - 08/01/2018

Two Big Economic Questions

Can the pursuit of self-interest promote the social interest?
People need to make economic choices that result in what, how and for whom goods and services are produced every day.

  • Do we produce the right things in the right quantities?
  • Do we use our factors of production in the best way?
  • Do the goods and services go to those who should get them?

Self-Interest: the choice is the best one available for you

Social Interest: the choice is the best for society as a whole

Efficient: it in is not possible to make someone better without making some else worse, then the resource use is efficient; otherwise, not efficient

Expropriation of Private Property
This happens when the government takes private property for public purpose.
For example, the government wants to take the private land for building a road.
The land owner will get "compensated", but not as much as he/she would like to have.
The government will offer the market price for buying the land from the owner.
However, the owner may not want to sell the land; he/she may want more that the market price.
Of course, the owner may have his/her own personal reason for not selling the land.
The government may force to take the land because of social interest.
Is this fair? Is it in the social interest?
This is why not everyone agree with all social interest.

It is hard to answer the questions about the social interest. Today, social interest forces on four different issues:

  • Globalizaion: the expansion of international trade, borrowing and lending, and investment
  • Information-Age Monopolie: the technological change of the past 40 years has been called the Information Revoluion
  • Climate Change
  • Economic Instability

The Economic Way of Thinking
  • a choice is a tradeoff
  • people make rational choices (or "purposeful") by comparing benefits and costs
  • benefit is what you gain from something
  • cost is what you must give up to get something
  • most choices are "how much" choices made at the margin
  • choices respond to incentives

People will make the choice which is better of themselves, but not for the society.


Thinking about Our Actions as Choices

Example: Going to university for 4+ years as a choice

What will we give up?
Time, the salary we can get from a job, ...
These are our opportunity cost.

What do we expect benefits?
Knowledges, a better salary after graduation, ...
These are our expected benefits.


  • Opportunity Cost
    • the highest-valued alternative that must be given up for getting something
  • Rational Choice
    • it compares costs and benefits and achieves the greatest benefit over cost for the person making the choice
    • it does NOT mean smart choice
    • it is the choice that makes people feel good

Only the wants of the person making a choice are relevant to determine its rationality.

Question: What goods and services will be produced and in what quantities?
Answer: Those that people rationally choice to buy!


Economics: A Social Science and Policy Tool

Economists are the social scientists; they distinguish between two types of statements:

  • Positive Statements: What is
    • can be tested by checking it against facts
  • Normative Statements: What ought to be
    • expresses an opinion and cannot be tested

Understand the Cause and the Effect
The task of economic science is to discover positive statements that are consistent with what we observe in the world and that enable us to understand how the economic world works.
Economists create and test economic models.

  • Economic Model
    • a description of some aspect of the economic world that includes only those features that are needed for the purpose at hand

Economists are also the policy advisors.
Economics is a set of tools for advising governments, businesses and for making personal decisions.
All the policy questions on which economists provide advice involve a blend of the positive and the normative.
Economics can't help with the normative part (the goal).
But for a given goal, economics provides a method of evaluating alternative solutions — comparing marginal benefits and marginal costs.

Chapter 2 - The Economic Problem

In election campaigns, candidates often make a lot of promises, which people want to have/change.
For example, more and better health care, better roads/bridges/sewer systems, better education, etc.

The concept of Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF) helps us to understand why these promises are unrealistic.


The Story behind the Production Possibilities Frontier

There is a limited amount of factors of production (land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship) in an economy anytime.
For example, time is limited - you may focus on one course and get a high mark, but the other courses will not do well.

The factors of production can be employed to produce goods and services that people want.
For example, we think more pizza should be produced in Canada.
Thus, we need to employ more factors of production in the production of pizza.
That is, more land for pizza restaurants, more people to build new restaurants, more machines, more backers, etc.
But we cannot create something out of nothing, the additional resources will have to come from other places.
Assume there are only two industries: pizza and cola.
Then the additional resources will come from the production of cola.
